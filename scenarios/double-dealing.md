# Scenario: Double Dealing

## Narrative
The Kepler Compact and the Voss Extraction Consortium have been bleeding each other dry over helium-3 rights in the Jovian trailing trojans for the better part of a decade. Skirmishes, embargoes, proxy sabotage — the usual. But the coffers are running low on both sides, and someone blinked first. They've agreed to negotiate a resource-sharing treaty at Meridian Station, a neutral commerce hub in the mid-belt with no formal allegiance to either faction. Mediators have been appointed. Delegations have arrived. The docking ring is full of ships flying colors that don't usually sit this close without shooting.

You got here three weeks before anyone else. That was the plan. You spent the first week building trust with the Compact's chief negotiator, Alara Maren — careful conversations, a shared drink, an offhand comment about a supply route vulnerability the Consortium might exploit. She started asking for your read on the Consortium's intentions. By the time the Consortium delegation arrived, you were already positioned. Their lead strategist, Dom Kazarek, needed local intelligence and a back-channel to gauge how serious the Compact was about concessions. You offered both. Neither side ran a background check deep enough to find the other connection — or maybe they did and assumed they could use you better than the other side could.

Now you're sitting in the middle of the highest-stakes negotiation this sector has seen in years, and both sides think you're exclusively theirs. Maren briefs you on the Compact's bottom line every evening in a private suite on Deck 14. Kazarek meets you in a rented cargo office on Deck 6 every morning to discuss pressure tactics. You carry secrets in both directions — selectively, carefully, never enough to blow your cover but always enough to stay indispensable. The pay is extraordinary. The danger is worse. Each side has security teams running counter-intelligence. Journalists are circling. And the longer the negotiations drag on, the more chances someone notices that you always seem to know what the other side is about to propose before they propose it.

One slipped detail. One overlapping schedule. One NPC who sees you leaving the wrong deck at the wrong hour. That's all it takes.

## Setting
- **Location type**: station (interior)
- **Scale**: regional
- **Tone**: political, espionage

## Player Agency
- **Broker genuine peace**: Use your unique position to steer both sides toward a fair treaty — reveal your dual role strategically, or keep it hidden and nudge from the shadows
- **Manipulate for profit**: Feed each side just enough real intelligence to stay trusted, extract maximum payment from both, and disappear before the treaty collapses or your cover breaks
- **Kingmaker**: Choose a side and use the intelligence from the other to give your chosen faction a decisive advantage in negotiations
- **Double agent indefinitely**: Maintain the con as long as possible, accepting escalating risk for escalating reward — every cycle you survive undetected increases your payout but narrows your escape window
- **Sell out to a third party**: A journalist, a rival faction, or a corporate intelligence broker would pay handsomely for what you know about both sides' positions
- **Burn it all down**: Leak both sides' secrets simultaneously and watch the negotiations implode — some people profit from chaos, and a failed treaty means the conflict resumes, which is good for arms dealers, mercenaries, and opportunists
- **Come clean**: Confess to one or both sides and attempt to survive the fallout — honesty might earn a strange kind of respect, or it might earn a spacing

## Resolution Paths
- **Best**: Broker a fair treaty using your knowledge of both sides' true positions — both factions get an acceptable deal, and you emerge as the architect of peace (whether or not anyone knows the full truth of how you did it)
- **Good**: Successfully play both sides to completion and extract maximum payment, vanishing before anyone uncovers the deception — wealthy but burned in this sector
- **Mixed**: Forced to pick a side when exposure threatens — you survive and your chosen faction benefits, but the other becomes a permanent enemy and the treaty is lopsided
- **Bad**: Exposed mid-negotiation — trust collapses on both sides, the treaty fails, and you're detained by station security while both factions demand extradition
- **Worst**: Both factions independently discover your betrayal and coordinate against you — hunted on a station with no allies, no faction willing to harbor you, and security closing in

## Required Simulation Systems

### Diplomacy & Negotiation
- **Purpose**: Simulates the formal and informal negotiation process between two factions, including proposals, counter-proposals, red lines, concessions, and backroom dealing
- **Key State**: Treaty terms under discussion, each faction's public position vs. private bottom line, concessions offered/demanded, negotiation phase (opening, mid-stage, endgame), mediator credibility, deadlock status, backroom deals in progress, information asymmetry between parties
- **Player-facing**: Briefing sessions with each faction's negotiator, ability to suggest proposals or poison-pill terms, visibility into how close each side is to walking away, tension indicators when contradictory advice starts surfacing in formal sessions

### Faction System
- **Purpose**: Simulates the two primary factions as complex organizations with internal politics, competing priorities, and members who may not all agree with the leadership's strategy
- **Key State**: Faction name, leadership hierarchy, delegation roster, internal hawks vs. doves, faction resources and war-readiness, historical grievances, alliance networks, splinter risk, delegation members' individual agendas
- **Player-facing**: Dialogue that reflects faction culture and priorities, internal faction disagreements the player can exploit or heal, faction members who might be turned or who might independently discover the player's double role

### Reputation & Trust
- **Purpose**: Tracks the intricate web of trust, suspicion, and perceived loyalty that determines whether the player's double game holds together or unravels
- **Key State**: Trust level per individual (Maren, Kazarek, their deputies, security chiefs, station contacts), suspicion triggers (contradictory advice traced back to player, schedule anomalies, witness sightings), trust decay rate when the player misses meetings or delivers bad intelligence, cover story consistency, exposure risk score
- **Player-facing**: Subtle dialogue shifts when trust erodes — questions that probe a little deeper, requests to explain where you were, NPCs testing you with false information to see if it reaches the other side, mounting paranoia as suspicion rises

### Economy & Currency
- **Purpose**: Simulates the financial dimensions of the negotiation and the player's personal profit motive — payments from both factions, bribes to maintain cover, and the economic stakes of the treaty itself
- **Key State**: Player payment ledger (per faction), payment schedule, bribe costs for station contacts, cost of maintaining separate secure meeting spaces, treaty economic terms (resource rights, trade routes, revenue splits), black market rates for leaked intelligence, escape fund threshold
- **Player-facing**: Dual income streams with visible risk-reward tradeoff, escalating costs to maintain the deception (bribing witnesses, renting secure comms, paying for counter-surveillance), a running calculation of how much you've earned vs. how much you need to disappear cleanly

### NPC Identity & Personality
- **Purpose**: Gives each key NPC a distinct personality, agenda, perceptiveness, and breaking point that determines how they respond to the player's manipulation and how likely they are to detect the deception
- **Key State**: Name, role, faction allegiance, personality traits (trusting/paranoid, methodical/impulsive), personal agenda beyond faction goals, perceptiveness rating, information held, information shared with player, suspicion level, relationship to other NPCs, what it would take to buy their silence or earn their loyalty
- **Player-facing**: Maren's careful probing vs. Kazarek's blunt demands for results, deputies who resent the player's access, a security officer who keeps showing up where you don't want them, NPCs whose personal ambitions you can exploit to deflect suspicion

### Communications
- **Purpose**: Simulates the flow of information across secure and insecure channels — the lifeblood of the player's double game and the primary vector for exposure
- **Key State**: Encrypted channel access (per faction), message logs, dead drop locations, comms intercept capability per faction, signal monitoring by station security, information compartmentalization (who knows what), rumor propagation among delegation staff, journalist monitoring of public channels
- **Player-facing**: Managing separate encrypted channels without cross-contamination, dead drops for sensitive exchanges, risk of comms being intercepted or logged, ability to plant disinformation, journalists who piece together patterns from public comms traffic

### Security & Threat
- **Purpose**: Simulates counter-intelligence operations by both factions and station security — the active threat of discovery
- **Key State**: Faction security team roster and capability, counter-intelligence operations active, surveillance targets (including potentially the player), sweep schedules, station security alert level, tail detection (is the player being followed), secure room sweep status, physical evidence of the player's movements
- **Player-facing**: Counter-intel operatives who shadow delegation members, secure rooms that get swept for bugs, faction security running background checks that might connect the dots, station security cameras the player must account for, the creeping realization that someone is asking questions about you

### Station Administration
- **Purpose**: Simulates Meridian Station's governance as a neutral host with its own interests in the treaty's success or failure and the authority to detain, expel, or protect the player
- **Key State**: Station administrator identity, neutrality policy, economic interest in treaty outcome, security force disposition, docking authority, diplomatic immunity rules, station reputation at stake, willingness to intervene if negotiations collapse, pressure from external powers
- **Player-facing**: Station officials who want the treaty to succeed for economic reasons and may help or hinder the player accordingly, administrative authority invoked if the player's activities threaten the station's neutral status, docking controls that could trap the player if things go wrong

### Social Dynamics
- **Purpose**: Simulates the informal social ecosystem of the station during the negotiations — the bars, the lobbies, the corridors where delegates mingle and gossip flows freely
- **Key State**: Social gathering locations, who is seen talking to whom, gossip propagation network, social events (receptions, dinners, informal meetings), NPC social observations (who noticed the player where), delegate social circles, journalist social mining, alibi plausibility
- **Player-facing**: The danger of being seen in the wrong place at the wrong time, social events where both factions are present and the player must navigate without revealing dual allegiance, gossip that reaches the wrong ears, NPCs who casually mention seeing you on a deck you shouldn't have been on

### Time Pressure
- **Purpose**: Simulates the ticking clock of the negotiations and the compounding risk of maintaining the double game over time — every cycle that passes increases both the payoff and the probability of exposure
- **Key State**: Negotiation timeline (days remaining), treaty deadline, faction patience thresholds, escalating suspicion over time, payment milestones, exposure probability curve, escape windows (ship departures, docking schedules), point-of-no-return markers
- **Player-facing**: A visible negotiation clock counting down, faction impatience if the player stalls, payment milestones that tempt the player to stay one more cycle, narrowing escape windows as station security tightens for the treaty signing, the gut-level tension of knowing every day you stay is a day closer to getting caught

## System Dependencies

### Hard Dependencies
- `Diplomacy & Negotiation` --depends on--> `Faction System`: treaty negotiations are defined by the two factions' interests, structures, and power dynamics
- `Security & Threat` --depends on--> `Faction System`: counter-intelligence operations are run by faction security teams with faction-specific priorities
- `Communications` --depends on--> `Station Administration`: communication infrastructure is controlled by the station and subject to its monitoring policies

### Soft Dependencies
- `Reputation & Trust` ~~depends on~~> `NPC Identity & Personality`: trust levels are shaped by individual NPC perceptiveness, personality, and personal agendas
- `Reputation & Trust` ~~depends on~~> `Social Dynamics`: being seen in the wrong place or with the wrong people erodes trust and triggers suspicion
- `Reputation & Trust` ~~depends on~~> `Communications`: intercepted messages or communication pattern analysis can expose the double game
- `Security & Threat` ~~depends on~~> `Reputation & Trust`: rising suspicion triggers counter-intelligence operations targeting the player
- `Security & Threat` ~~depends on~~> `Communications`: surveillance of communication channels is a primary counter-intelligence tool
- `Diplomacy & Negotiation` ~~depends on~~> `Reputation & Trust`: the player's influence in negotiations depends on maintained trust with both sides
- `Diplomacy & Negotiation` ~~depends on~~> `Economy & Currency`: treaty terms revolve around economic resource allocation
- `Economy & Currency` ~~depends on~~> `Time Pressure`: payments increase with duration, but so do costs of maintaining cover
- `Social Dynamics` ~~depends on~~> `NPC Identity & Personality`: social observations and gossip are driven by individual NPC behavior and awareness
- `Time Pressure` ~~depends on~~> `Diplomacy & Negotiation`: the negotiation timeline defines the overall clock and pacing of the scenario
- `Station Administration` ~~depends on~~> `Diplomacy & Negotiation`: station governance is invested in the treaty outcome and responds to negotiation developments

### Feedback Loops
- `Reputation & Trust` <-> `Security & Threat`: Suspicion triggers counter-intel operations; counter-intel findings further erode trust; eroded trust causes faction handlers to test the player with false information, generating more security activity
- `Diplomacy & Negotiation` <-> `Reputation & Trust` <-> `Communications`: Player advice shapes negotiation positions; when advice proves suspiciously accurate, trust erodes; factions tighten communication security, reducing the player's access to intelligence, which reduces their value as an advisor
- `Time Pressure` <-> `Reputation & Trust` <-> `Economy & Currency`: Each passing cycle raises the payout but compounds exposure risk; the player must decide whether the next payment milestone is worth the narrowing margin of safety; delayed departure increases costs (bribes, counter-surveillance) that eat into profits
- `Social Dynamics` <-> `NPC Identity & Personality` <-> `Reputation & Trust`: NPCs observe the player's movements and share observations socially; gossip reaches faction security; the player must manage social appearances while maintaining contradictory relationships, and a single witnessed corridor encounter can unravel weeks of careful positioning

## Emergent Possibilities
- A junior delegate from one faction develops a personal attachment to the player and begins independently investigating why the player seems to know so much — their snooping might expose the double game, or the player might turn them into a willing accomplice
- Both factions' security teams independently begin surveilling the player on the same day, and a near-miss where two tails almost spot each other forces the player into an improvised cover story that creates a third layer of deception
- The treaty negotiations stall over an issue the player could resolve by revealing information they're not supposed to have — forcing a choice between maintaining cover and achieving a genuine diplomatic breakthrough that could save lives
- A journalist pieces together the player's meeting patterns from public deck access logs and approaches the player with a deal: exclusive story rights in exchange for not publishing immediately — but the journalist's sources might include someone from one of the factions

## Inspiration
- *Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy* (film/novel) — the paranoia of operating inside overlapping intelligence operations where trust is currency and exposure is death
- *The Expanse* — Avasarala's multilateral political maneuvering, back-channel diplomacy, and the personal cost of playing factions against each other
- *Bridge of Spies* (film) — negotiating between hostile powers from a position of manufactured neutrality
- *Babylon 5* — Londo and G'Kar's faction dynamics, station-based diplomacy where personal relationships determine political outcomes
- *Deus Ex* (game) — navigating between factions who each believe you're working for them, with information as the primary weapon
